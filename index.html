Before anything else

One rule that makes these scams harder to pull off

Remember one thing and do it every time.

The household rule
If anyone contacts you unexpectedly about your money or your accounts, and you are not 100% certain you know this person from a previous in-person meeting or prior phone conversation, hang up. Then call back using a number you already know is real.
Number on the back of your debit card
Number on the back of your credit card
Number you have stored in your phone
Number you looked up yourself
Never call the number they give you.

These scams are engineered to trick intelligent people. The urgency is fake. The pressure is a tool for making you act before you have time to think. The moment you feel rushed to act on a financial call you did not initiate, that feeling itself is the warning sign.

It is always okay to hang up. It is always okay to check the number and call back. No legitimate bank, government agency, or financial institution will ever penalize you for taking 60 seconds to verify before you act.

You have worked too hard for too long to lose it to a phone call.

Know the playbook

Here is exactly how these scams work

Every one of these scams follows the same script: create urgency, create fear, keep you from verifying, move the money fast. Once you know what to listen for, you can recognize it.

Your phone rings. The caller ID shows your bank's name and real phone number. The person on the line sounds professional and urgent. They tell you there is suspicious activity on your account and they need to verify your identity to stop it.

They already know things. Your name. Your address. Maybe the last four digits of your account. This makes them sound legitimate. It may not be. Criminals buy this information. It is widely available.

Then they make their move. Confirm your password. Read back a code they just texted you. Or move your money to a secure account they set up for you while they investigate.

A real bank will never tell you to move your money to a new account to protect it. That account belongs to the criminal.

Someone contacts you, sometimes through WhatsApp, sometimes through a phone call or text, claiming there is a problem with your account or your device. They offer to help. To do that, they need you to share your screen or install a small app so they can walk you through the fix.

Once you do that, they can see everything on your screen in real time. Your banking app. Your passwords as you type them. The one-time codes your bank sends to confirm transactions. They watch, they capture, and the money moves.

The scam is not limited to WhatsApp. It is about screen access. It works on any platform, through any app, on any device.

Never share your screen with someone who contacted you unexpectedly. Never install an app because a stranger told you to. Never let someone walk you through moving money.

Any caller who pressures you to stay on the line, act immediately, or not call anyone else is telling you something important. Legitimate organizations do not operate that way.

Hang up. Look up the real phone number yourself — on the back of your card, on the official website, or on a statement you already have. Call that number. Not the one the caller gave you.

Do this before anything else happens. Before you move money. Before you share information. Before you do anything they asked. One phone call to a number you found yourself is the single most effective thing you can do.

What to do this week

Five things I would tell my own family to do right now

None of these require a tech background. All of them are free or close to it. Together they make it significantly harder for anyone to get into your accounts or open new ones in your name.

  • 1
    Freeze your credit at all three bureaus This locks your credit file so no one can open new accounts in your name. Free, does not affect your credit score, and does not affect any accounts you already have. Do it at each bureau separately: Equifax (equifax.com), Experian (experian.com), TransUnion (transunion.com). Takes about 15 minutes total. When you need to apply for credit later, you lift it temporarily and then put it back.
  • 2
    Check your credit reports Go to annualcreditreport.com, the only federally authorized site. You can now check your report from each bureau once a week for free. Look for accounts you do not recognize, addresses you have never lived at, and inquiries you did not authorize. Any of those is a flag.
  • 3
    Turn on multi-factor authentication everywhere This means your bank, your email, your investment accounts, and your phone carrier all require a second step to log in, usually a code texted to your phone. Even if a criminal gets your password, they cannot get in without that code. Most apps have this under Settings, then Security or Privacy. Never read that code out loud to anyone who called you.
  • 4
    Use a password manager Reused passwords are the single biggest vulnerability most people have. If you use the same password at multiple sites and one of those sites gets hacked, criminals try that password everywhere else. A password manager creates a unique strong password for every account and remembers them all. We use 1Password. Individual plan is 7.88 per year. Family plan for up to five people is 1.88. Free 14-day trial at 1password.com.
  • 5
    Save your bank's real fraud number right now Find it on the back of your debit or credit card and save it as a contact in your phone. Label it clearly. If you ever get a call claiming to be your bank, hang up and call that saved number yourself. Do not use the number the caller gives you. Do not stay on the line. It is always okay to be firm with someone who is pressuring you.
If it has already happened

Here is exactly what to do and in what order

The order matters. When money is actively moving, every minute counts.

Act immediately

Call your bank first

Call your bank's fraud line. The number is on the back of your debit or credit card. If money is still moving, they may be able to stop or reverse it. Ask them to flag or freeze the transaction.

Then call Trent

Report and document

After your accounts are secured

File a police report if money was taken. You may need it for extended fraud alerts and to support any dispute process with your bank. Then report to the appropriate agencies.

  • Local police: file a report and keep a copy
  • IdentityTheft.gov: for identity theft specifically. Generates a personalized step-by-step recovery plan
  • ReportFraud.ftc.gov: for scams and fraud broadly, including bank impersonation and messaging app scams
  • FBI IC3 (ic3.gov): for wire transfer fraud and cybercrime
  • Virginia Attorney General Consumer Protection Hotline: 800-552-9963, Monday through Friday 8:30am to 5pm
